Intuition behind decay of Fourier coefficients

up vote 2 down vote favorite
2

Many other posts have discussed the standard result that the smoothness of a function is related to the rate at which its Fourier coefficients decay. For example, there are proofs that show that if f is of class Ck then |cn| tends to zero faster than |n|−k as n→±∞.

Is there a intuitive geometric picture to explain this? If f is not differentiable imagine slowly changing the function so that it becomes twice differentiable, then the Fourier coefficients cn=12π∫π−πf(θ)e−inθdθ should change their rate of decay, but how is that obvious from the definition of the Fourier coefficients.

What about considering cn(θ)=12π∫θ−πf(ϕ)e−inϕdϕ. Is there some kind of bad behavior in cn(θ) when it encounters a discontinuity in f(k)(θ) that would prevent cn(θ) from being small.

It really boils down to integration by parts.. the nth Fourier coefficient of dkfdtk is given by (in)kf^(n), which you prove by integrating by parts k times. If f is Ck, then the Fourier coefficients of the continuous function dkfdtk go to zero by the Riemann-Lebesgue lemma, so you get that |n|k|f^(n)| goes to zero or that |f^(n)| is o(1|n|k).

If you have a small discontinuity in the kth derivative, you might get a small error term like ϵ1|n|k−12 just to give an example, and as the discontinuity goes to zero the ϵ will go to zero... but for any ϵ>0 it will still be the dominant term if |n| is large enough. So slowly changing the function will gradually make this term go away in one sense, but it will still dominate in another sense regardless of what ϵ is.
